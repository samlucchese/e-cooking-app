What is food and kitchen safety?



Every year, 1 in 6 people become sick from contaminated food, and there are many others who think that food safety and kitchen safety are the same thing. However, while they both are equally important, they each address different things. Food safety refers to the proper handling and storage of your food so that it doesnt make you sick, while kitchen safety refers to the cleanliness of your environment and the way you handle your tools. Both of these things go hand-in-hand, and it is very important to understand each one in order to ensure safe cooking.


Why is it important?



This question seems pretty straightforward right? Safety in the kitchen is important because nobody wants to slice off a finger, slip and fall, or wake up the next day with food poisoning. However, accidents do happen, and we should be prepared to handle these accidents and learn from our mistakes to prevent future ones. The number one reason for accidents in the kitchen is carelessness. Learning to slow things down and not rush through the recipe will help you to keep your mind on the task at hand. This will not only prevent accidents, but it will ensure that you do not miss a crucial step that could ruin your meal.

    How to Practice Safety



    There are two different ways we can practice safety, with our food, and with our equipment.

    Food Safety

    Clean

    • Wash your hands with soapy warm water before and after you finish cooking. Clean all utensils and cooking surfaces before you begin. Wash any fruits and vegetables you have under running water.

    Separate

    • Keep fresh produce away from raw meat, chicken, turkey, seafood, and eggs, and use separate cutting boards for each meat. These foods can spread germs to each other and become contaminated. Store raw meats in the bottom drawer of your refrigerator to reduce contamination if the juices from the meats drip.

    Cook

    • Cook food to the correct internal temperature. It is important so that the food is cooked long enough to not make us sick. The only surefire way to know your food's internal temperature is by using an instant-read thermometer. See grilling course for more information.

    Chill

    • Refrigerate any perishable leftovers within 2 hours. (1 hour if the temperature outside is 90°F or warmer). The reason for this is because bacteria can multiply rapidly if left in the Danger Zone of 40°F and 140°F.
    • Thaw food safely by placing it in the refrigerator, in cold water, or in the microwave. Never thaw food at room temperature, as bacteria will multiply much more quickly in the parts that reach room temperature first.


    Kitchen Safety

    Knives

    • Keep your knives sharp and store them in a wooden knife block or a drawer, out of reach of children.
    • When slicing, slice away from your hand and keep your fingers curled back while holding the food underneath. In the rare case of an accident, the knife will bounce off of your knuckles easier than your fingertips.
    • Use a cutting board, and never slice anything over the sink.

    Stovetop

    • Turn pot and pan handles away from the front of the stove so you don't bump and spill anything.
    • Always double check that you turned the stove or oven off when you are finished cooking.

    Attire

    • Make sure you are wearing an apron. If you don't have an apron, wear clothes that cover your body in the event of an accident.
    • Keep your hair tied back so there is no risk of it falling into the food (or worse, catching on fire...)

    Lastly...

    • Clean up spills and messes as you go. One of the greatest things I've learned is that cleaning as you go not only prevents accidents, but also saves a significant amount of time in the clean-up process.
    • Don't rush. Rushing around in the kitchen only increases the chances of an accident happening. Taking your time on and in-between each step of the recipe will always take less time than if you had to make a trip to the hospital.
